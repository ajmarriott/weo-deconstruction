Turkey Heading for a Train Wreck
June 2016 Scott Ritter


Recent developments in Turkey have reinforced concerns in the West that Turkish democracy is being sacrificed in favor of autocratic rule. The passage of a constitutional amendment that revokes the immunity of members of parliament opens the door for those suspended to be prosecuted under Turkey's broad anti-terrorism laws. This slide toward authoritarianism has created a tense domestic political environment that is further exacerbated by deteriorating relations between Turkey and its Kurdish minority. The increasing instability pushes Turkey closer to civil war and economic crisis -- precursors for the kind of military intervention into Turkish politics that many observers believed had been relegated to the past, but which now has re-entered the realm of the possible. The repercussions from this growing internal instability are enormous for Turkey's relations with both its regional neighbors and allies, not to mention the critical role it plays as an energy transit state.

The recent decision by Turkey's president, Recep Tayyip Erdogan, together with the ruling Justice and Development Party (AKP), to unilaterally broaden the definition of terrorism to include so-called "supporters" of terrorism, whether they are journalists, members of parliament, or members of civil society -- even if they are not directly involved in any kind of terror activity -- has all but stymied an already contested effort by Turkey to join the EU. This issue has become even more complicated in light of the ongoing debate between the EU and Turkey over the issue of refugee migration, and the recent passage by the Turkish parliament of a new constitutional amendment, pushed through by the ruling AKP, intended to specifically target those members of parliament who belong to the pro-Kurdish People's Democratic Party (HDP) on the grounds that they are supporters of the Kurdistan Worker's Party, or PKK, which has been waging a low-grade civil war inside Turkey for Kurdish rights.

A deal was reached this past April between Turkey and the EU to help forestall the flood of refugees, primarily but not exclusively originating as a result of the ongoing conflict inside Syria, Iraq and Afghanistan, coming into Europe via the Turkish land bridge connecting Europe with Asia. The deal, in which the EU provided Turkey with $6.6 billion, plus promises of accelerated EU membership talks and conditional visa-free travel for Turkish citizens in Europe, in exchange for absorbing refugees returned from Europe and shutting down networks used to smuggle refugees into Europe, was heavily criticized from its inception by those in the EU who questioned Turkey's human rights record and ongoing crackdown on domestic dissent. The recently passed constitutional amendment lifting parliamentarian immunity, when combined with Turkey's violent suppression of Kurdish unrest inside Turkey, have provided ammunition to those in the EU who have insisted on meaningful political reforms inside Turkey, especially regarding human rights, before any visa-waiver policy could be enacted. The resulting impasse between Turkey and the EU led to the resignation of Turkish Prime Minister Ahmet Davutoglu, who negotiated the deal, and threats from Erdogan that the entire deal may be scrapped as a result, further straining the already tense relations between Turkey and the EU.

The biggest domestic problem for Turkey, however, is the Kurdish question. Erdogan has been walking a political tightrope with the Kurds since 2002, when he swept into office as Turkey's prime minister in the aftermath of the AKP's electoral victory. Erdogan took steps to liberalize Turkey's Kurdish policies, opening the door for more formal Kurdish involvement in Turkey's domestic political process. While Erdogan and the AKP had hoped Turkey's Kurds would align with them politically, the Kurds instead pursued an independent direction, with Kurdish politicians winning elections at the municipality level in 2004 and again in 2009. In 2012, the various Kurdish political factions, together with left-wing progressive Turks, combined to create the People's Democratic Party, or HDP, in an effort to break the 10% threshold in national elections required to seat representatives at the Turkish parliament. That same year, Erdogan began peace talks with the PKK, which were brokered by the HDP. In 2015, the HDP won 13.9% of the vote in Turkey's presidential election, making it the third-largest political party in Turkey and granting political entrée into both the parliament and the AKP-dominated government.

The HDP-AKP relationship was tenuous from the start. The collapse of the peace talks between Ankara and the PKK in the summer of 2015, when combined with the deteriorating situation inside Syria and Iraq, where Kurdish minorities have sought unprecedented autonomy, resulted in an ever-expanding conflict between Turkish security forces and the PKK, both inside Turkey and across the borders, in Syria and Iraq. This conflict has hardened the respective positions of Ankara, which seeks Kurdish integration into a Turkish-dominated society, and the PKK-Kurds, who seek increased autonomy and eventual independence. The HDP was caught in the middle, and paid the price.

The efforts of the AKP to criminally prosecute members of the HDP accused of helping the PKK paves the way toward the elimination of enough HDP parliamentarians to put the HDP below the 10% threshold, thereby removing all HDP members from parliament and allowing the vacated seats to be assumed by the majority AKP. In addition to further alienating Turkey's already restive Kurdish population, such a move would pave the way for the accession of even greater powers by Erdogan. Currently, the AKP lacks the parliamentarian majority required to muscle through constitutional amendments, imbuing the Turkish presidency with unprecedented political powers that would transform Turkey from a parliamentarian democracy to one run by an all-powerful executive. If the AKP can seize control of the HDP seats, however, it will have such a majority, and Erdogan has made it clear he will be seeking the amendments to the Turkish constitution required for power to be consolidated in his office. An all-powerful Turkish presidency, given Erdogan's autocratic bent, can only deepen the divide between Turkey and the Kurds, and Turkey and the EU.

Popular opinion inside Turkey and without holds that the days of Turkish military intervention into Turkish domestic politics are over. President Erdogan has waged a forceful domestic campaign to stifle the politicization of the Turkish military, and to date the Turkish General Staff (the traditional incubator of coups in the past) has endorsed this posture. Nevertheless, the Turkish military continues to embrace as a core mission, bestowed upon it by Mustafa Kemal Ataturk, the founder of the modern Turkish state, as the defender of the Turkish constitution. If extreme conditions of ideological and/or societal fragmentation were to become manifest, or if Turkey were to suffer economic collapse or military defeat as a result of inept civilian leadership, then the Turkish military could very well reconsider its current ban on involving itself in politics. The fact of the matter is that in Turkey today there is the real potential for one or more of these preconditions for military intervention to occur.

Erdogan has displayed an Islamic bent that runs against the grain of the kind of secular Kemalism espoused by the military hierarchy. If Erdogan were able to further consolidate his political position by significantly changing the constitution of Turkey, and in doing so pushed Islamist policies to an extreme, there would be the real possibility of engendering the kind of significant ideological opposition that could lead to the fragmentation of Turkish society akin to that which existed in the 1970s, prior to the coup of 1980. More significantly, Erdogan has pushed Turkey down the path toward both civil and regional conflict with the Kurds, creating a situation where the only political option besides engaging in an endless war that cannot be won would be the partition of Turkey, something the Turkish military would never agree to.

The drive for authoritarian rule that Erdogan is currently embarked on has Turkey on the path toward political crisis, economic ruin, civil and regional war, and, in the end, the death of the very democratic institutions Erdogan has tried to strengthen, for better or for worse, since coming to power in 2002. Void of an unforeseen swing in public opinion that ejects Erdogan and the AKP from political power, there appears to be nothing that can stop this Turkish train wreck before it is too late. Even a military coup, should one come about, would only be triggered once the situation in Turkey had deteriorated well past the point of no return. For the US, EU and NATO, Turkey's days as a reliable political and economic ally are numbered. It is no longer a question of if Turkey will fail but when.

Scott Ritter is a former US Marine Corps intelligence officer and arms control specialist with on-site inspection experience inside the former Soviet Union and Iraq. He is the author of "Target Iran," published by Nation Books.
